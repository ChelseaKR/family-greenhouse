# Common houseplant pests

Four pests cause 90% of houseplant pest problems indoors. They all spread from one plant to another, so when you find any of them, isolate the infested plant from the rest.

## Spider mites

Tiny (0.5mm) arachnids — not insects. The clearest signal is fine webbing between leaves and stems. They thrive in dry air and quickly destroy plants in heated winter homes.

- **What you see**: stippled, dusty-looking leaves; fine webbing on the underside; yellow speckles that look almost gold-flecked
- **Where they hide**: underside of leaves, leaf axils
- **What to do**: shower the plant in lukewarm water (literal shower — they hate water), every 3 days for 2 weeks. Add neem oil or insecticidal soap between showers. Raise the humidity to make the environment hostile.

Spider mites are the hardest pest to eradicate. You will think you got them; you didn't. Treat for a full 3 weeks after the last sighting.

## Mealybugs

Small white cottony-looking insects that cluster in leaf joints. They suck sap and excrete a sticky honeydew that grows sooty mold.

- **What you see**: white fluff in the crooks of stems and leaves, leaves looking sticky or shiny, ants crawling on the plant (they farm the honeydew)
- **What to do**: spot-treat with a Q-tip dipped in rubbing alcohol. The alcohol dissolves their waxy coating instantly; they're dead the moment you touch them. Follow up with neem oil weekly until you've gone 3 weeks without seeing one.

Mealybugs can be dormant in soil too. Check the root crown when repotting.

## Fungus gnats

Tiny black flies that crawl across the soil surface and buzz around when you water. The adults are annoying but harmless; the larvae eat root hairs.

- **What you see**: small black flies emerging when you water; the larvae are translucent worms 2-4mm long in the top inch of soil
- **What to do**: let the top inch of soil dry out completely between waterings (the larvae need moisture). Yellow sticky traps for the adults. For a heavy infestation, drench the soil with mosquito bits (BTI) — kills the larvae over a week.

Bottom-watering works very well against fungus gnats since the top stays dry.

## Thrips

The hardest pest to identify because they're tiny and elusive. Slim, fast-moving insects that scrape leaves and suck the sap, leaving silvery streaks and tiny black dots (their feces).

- **What you see**: silvery or bronze streaks on leaves; tiny black flecks; distorted new growth; leaves that look "scratched"
- **What to do**: heavy infestations need systemic insecticides (look for imidacloprid) or repeated applications of spinosad. Less-bad infestations: weekly insecticidal soap + sticky traps + clean off affected leaves.

Thrips can reproduce parthenogenically and have a fast life cycle. Don't stop treatment after one week.

## General pest hygiene

- **Quarantine new plants for 2 weeks** before introducing them to your collection. Most pest problems trace back to a new arrival.
- **Inspect undersides of leaves** when you water. That's where most pests hide.
- **Don't mist if you have spider mites** — water alone isn't enough and you'll spread spores. Use insecticidal soap instead.
- **Isolate infested plants** to a different room until 3 weeks past the last sighting.
