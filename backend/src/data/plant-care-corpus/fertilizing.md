# Fertilizing houseplants

Houseplants need fertilizer because their soil is a closed system — unlike outdoor soil, nothing's replenishing the nutrients as the plant uses them. But fertilizer is overdone far more often than underdone, and burned roots from over-fertilizing look a lot like underwatering.

## When to fertilize

- **Growing season (spring + summer)**: every 2-4 weeks at half the recommended strength.
- **Winter**: don't fertilize, or once every 6-8 weeks at quarter strength. Most houseplants slow down or go dormant when light drops.
- **Newly potted plants**: don't fertilize for 6-8 weeks after repotting. Fresh potting mix has nutrients already; adding more burns the still-recovering roots.
- **Stressed plants**: never fertilize a plant that's wilting, recently moved, or showing signs of root rot. Fix the underlying problem first.

## What "N-P-K" means

The three numbers on every fertilizer label: nitrogen-phosphorus-potassium.

- **N (nitrogen)**: leafy growth. Most houseplants are leaf-focused so a higher first number is good.
- **P (phosphorus)**: roots + flowers. Important when flowering or rooting cuttings.
- **K (potassium)**: overall plant vigor, disease resistance.

A balanced fertilizer (10-10-10 or 20-20-20) works for most houseplants. Specialty plants want specialty ratios — orchids want a 30-10-10 in growing season, then 10-10-30 to trigger blooming. Succulents want a low-nitrogen mix (like 2-7-7) so they don't get leggy.

## Half-strength rule

Almost every houseplant fertilizer label tells you to dilute to about 1 tsp per gallon (or whatever). Houseplants almost always do better at half that. The label is written assuming you're fertilizing tomatoes outside.

Half-strength every 2 weeks > full-strength every 4. Steady mild feeding beats infrequent shock feeding.

## Signs of under-fertilizing

- Pale leaves, especially new growth
- Slow or stalled growth despite good light + water
- Lower leaves yellowing while the rest of the plant looks fine
- Plant hasn't been fertilized in 6+ months

## Signs of over-fertilizing

- Crispy brown leaf tips (often confused with low humidity)
- White crust on the soil surface or pot rim (mineral salt buildup)
- Burnt-looking leaf edges
- Leaves dropping with no obvious other cause

If you suspect over-fertilizing, flush the soil: pour water through the pot 3-4 times the pot's volume to wash out salts. Skip the next 2 fertilizer cycles.

## Organic vs synthetic

Synthetic liquid fertilizers (Schultz, Miracle-Gro) act fast and are predictable. Organic options (worm castings, fish emulsion, compost tea) are gentler and slower. For indoor use synthetic is fine — organic fertilizers can smell, attract fungus gnats, and need to be turned into the soil.

Slow-release pellets (Osmocote) work for 3-6 months. Convenient for plants you'd otherwise forget to fertilize, but they release nutrients faster in heat — be careful in summer.
