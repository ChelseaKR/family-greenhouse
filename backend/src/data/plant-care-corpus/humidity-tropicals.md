# Humidity for tropical houseplants

Most tropical houseplants come from rainforests where humidity sits at 70-90%. Indoor air, especially in winter with the heat running, is often 20-30%. That's desert humidity.

## Which plants actually need high humidity

- **Calatheas, marantas, ctenanthes (prayer plants)**: 50%+ minimum, ideally 60-70%. They crisp up below 40%.
- **Ferns** (Boston, maidenhair, bird's nest): 50%+. Maidenhairs are notorious — they want 70%+ and turn to dust below that.
- **Anthuriums, alocasias**: 60%+.
- **Orchids** (most): 50-70%.
- **Calathea ornata, Calathea white fusion**: cry for the swamp; 70%+.

## Plants that don't care about humidity

- **Snake plants, ZZ plants, pothos, philodendrons, monsteras**: tolerate 30-40% just fine.
- **Cacti, succulents, jade**: prefer it dry.

If you're a beginner, start with plants that don't care about humidity. The plants on the "needs high humidity" list will frustrate you in a normal home until you've built up infrastructure.

## How to raise humidity

In order of effectiveness:

1. **Run a humidifier**. The only method that actually works in a dry room. A $40 cool-mist humidifier can hold a 200 sq ft room at 50-60%. Run it on a timer with the plants.
2. **Group plants together**. Plants transpire moisture from their leaves. A cluster of plants creates a microclimate noticeably wetter than the surrounding room.
3. **Move plants to the bathroom**. Bathrooms with a shower run a regular humidity cycle. Anthuriums and calatheas often do best on a bathroom shelf.
4. **Pebble trays**. A tray of pebbles with water under each pot. Adds maybe 5-10% locally. Marginal but free.
5. **Misting**. Mostly theater. The humidity bump lasts a few minutes. Doesn't hurt if you enjoy it but won't save a calathea.

## How to measure humidity

A $10 digital hygrometer from a hardware store. Put it next to the plants you care about. Most homes are drier than people guess — getting an actual number is more useful than misting "just in case".

## Signs of low humidity

- Crispy brown leaf tips, especially on calatheas
- Crispy edges curling inward
- Older fern fronds turning brown and crumbling
- Failure of new leaves to unfurl fully

Brown tips that look like they're literally sandpaper-textured are almost always humidity, not water. Crank up the humidifier before reaching for the watering can.
