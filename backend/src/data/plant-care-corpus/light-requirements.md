# Light requirements for houseplants

Light is the single most under-appreciated houseplant variable. Most "I'm not sure why my plant is dying" problems are actually light problems.

## What the light labels mean

- **Bright indirect**: within a few feet of a sunny window, but not in the path of direct beams. Most tropicals (monsteras, philodendrons, pothos, peace lilies, calatheas).
- **Direct sun**: in the path of sun for at least several hours a day. Most succulents, cacti, citrus, jade, string of pearls.
- **Medium light**: 5-10 feet from a sunny window, or a bright north-facing window. ZZ plants, snake plants, cast iron plants, philodendrons (they tolerate this even though they prefer bright indirect).
- **Low light**: more than 10 feet from any window, or a windowless bathroom. Very few plants actually thrive in true low light — pothos and snake plants tolerate it but grow slowly and stretch.

## How to measure light

Stand where your plant lives. If you can read a book without artificial light, it's at least medium. If you have to squint, it's low. If you can see a sharp shadow of your hand on the wall, it's direct sun.

A cheap light meter (or smartphone app) gives lux readings. Rough guidance:

- 200-500 lux: low
- 500-2000 lux: medium
- 2000-10000 lux: bright indirect
- 10000+ lux: direct sun

## Window directions (Northern Hemisphere)

- **South-facing**: brightest, most direct sun all day. Great for succulents, cacti, citrus. Tropicals burn here unless filtered with a sheer curtain.
- **East-facing**: gentle morning sun, ideal for most tropicals.
- **West-facing**: hot afternoon sun. Tougher on sensitive plants but good for medium-light lovers and succulents.
- **North-facing**: indirect light all day, no direct sun. Best for shade-tolerant plants.

## Signs of too little light

- Pale or yellowing new growth
- Leggy, stretched stems reaching toward the window
- Small leaves on new growth
- Slow or no growth
- Variegated plants losing their variegation (going all-green)

## Signs of too much light

- Scorched brown patches on leaves (especially on the side facing the window)
- Faded or washed-out leaf color
- Crispy, curled leaf edges
- Wilting that doesn't recover after watering

## Grow lights

If your apartment has no good window, a basic full-spectrum LED grow light from any hardware store solves the problem. Run 10-12 hours a day on a timer. Keep it 12-18 inches above the plant. Most tropicals are happy under a $30 grow light.
