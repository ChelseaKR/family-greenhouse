# Propagation basics

Propagation is how you turn one plant into many. For most popular houseplants it's straightforward and forgiving — even beginners get a high success rate.

## When to propagate

Spring through early fall, when the parent plant is actively growing. Cuttings root much faster in warm weather with long days. Winter cuttings often sit for weeks before rooting.

## Stem cuttings — the most common method

Works for: pothos, philodendrons, monsteras, hoyas, tradescantia, peperomia, ficus, jade, most tropical climbers.

1. **Find a node.** A node is the spot on the stem where a leaf attaches; it's where roots will grow from. You need at least one node, ideally two.
2. **Cut just below a node** with clean scissors. The cutting should have 2-4 leaves and at least one bare node.
3. **Remove the lowest leaf** so its node sits in water/soil without leaf rot.
4. **Choose a rooting medium**:
   - **Water**: clearest results, you can see the roots. Change water every 3-5 days. Transition to soil once roots are 2-3 inches.
   - **Damp sphagnum moss**: faster than water for many aroids. Keep in a clear container so you can watch progress.
   - **Direct to soil**: skip the water-rooting step. Stick cutting into damp well-draining soil, cover with a clear bag for humidity, place in indirect light.
5. **Wait**. Most cuttings root in 2-4 weeks. Some (monsteras, fiddle leaf figs) take 4-8.

## Leaf cuttings

Works for: snake plants, jade, African violets, peperomia, some succulents (echeverias, sedums).

For most leaf cuttings: just lay or stand the leaf on damp soil/perlite, keep barely moist, and a new plantlet emerges from the base in a few weeks. The original leaf eventually dies off after the baby is established.

Note: variegated snake plants and variegated monsteras propagated from leaf cuttings usually revert to all-green. Stem cuttings preserve variegation.

## Division

Works for: ZZ plants, snake plants, spider plants, calatheas, peace lilies, ferns, anything that grows in clumps.

When you unpot one of these and see multiple distinct plants sharing soil, you can separate them into individual pots. Each section needs roots + at least one growth point.

Best done when repotting anyway. Tease the root mass apart, cut through any sections you can't pull apart, repot each piece in its own appropriately-sized pot.

## Air layering

For plants too large to take cuttings from without damage (fiddle leaf figs, big rubber plants). You wound the stem partway up, wrap damp moss + plastic around the wound, and roots grow into the moss while the cutting is still attached to the parent. Once roots form, cut below the moss and pot it up.

More work than a stem cutting but very high success rate for difficult plants.

## When propagation fails

- **Cutting rotted in water**: water was stagnant or the cutting had no clean node. Change water more often; cut a fresh piece below the rotted part if salvageable.
- **No roots after a month**: light is too low (move to brighter indirect spot) or temperature is too cold (warm conditions speed rooting).
- **Roots formed but leaves yellowed when transferred to soil**: water roots and soil roots are structurally different; the cutting got overwhelmed. Acclimate slower — keep soil very moist for the first 2 weeks then taper.

## What you can sell, trade, or give away

A well-rooted cutting is a real gift. People love receiving propagated plants — there's a community around it (plant swaps, local groups). It's also a good way to test new soil mixes or care approaches on copies of your favorite plants before doing it to the original.
