# Root rot — how to spot it and what to do

Root rot is the silent killer of houseplants. By the time the symptoms are obvious aboveground, the roots are often 80% gone. Catching it early is the difference between a save and a goodbye.

## What it is

Root rot is a fungal infection (most commonly _Pythium_ or _Phytophthora_) that thrives in waterlogged soil with low oxygen. It eats the roots from the tips upward. Once it reaches the crown, the plant is unrecoverable.

The fungi are everywhere — in the air, the soil, on your fingers. They're harmless until conditions favor them: too much water, too little drainage, soil that stays soggy for days.

## Symptoms

Aboveground:

- Drooping despite wet soil (the classic tell)
- Yellow leaves, especially lower ones
- A musty, rotten smell from the soil
- Soft, mushy stem at the soil line
- Stunted or stopped growth

Belowground (you have to unpot to see):

- Healthy roots: white or cream, firm, branching
- Rotted roots: brown or black, mushy, easy to crush between your fingers, often smell foul
- Root ball is wet days after watering

## How to save a plant with root rot

1. **Unpot immediately**. Don't wait. Every day the rot spreads.
2. **Rinse the root ball** under tepid water. Be gentle but thorough — you need to see what you're working with.
3. **Cut off every rotten root.** Use clean scissors (wipe with rubbing alcohol between cuts). Trim back to white, firm tissue. Don't be precious — leaving even a small amount of rot reintroduces it.
4. **Trim foliage proportionally.** If you removed 50% of the roots, remove 30-50% of the leaves so the remaining roots aren't overwhelmed.
5. **Repot in fresh, dry, well-draining soil.** Don't reuse the old soil — it's infected. Use a small pot — over-potting is what caused this in the first place.
6. **Water sparingly** for the next few weeks. Wait until the top half of the soil is dry. The plant has less root mass and can't process as much water.

Survival rate depends on how much healthy root remained. >50% healthy roots: usually recovers. <30%: long shot, but propagation from a stem cutting may save the genetics.

## Prevention

- **Use a pot with drainage holes.** Always. Decorative no-hole pots are death traps.
- **Use well-draining soil.** Most generic "potting mix" is too dense for houseplants. Add 30-50% perlite or pumice.
- **Don't water on a schedule.** Water when the soil is dry, not when the calendar says so.
- **Don't size up the pot dramatically.** A 4" plant should go into a 5" or 6" pot, not a 10". Excess soil holds excess water.
- **Don't leave saucers full.** Empty them after watering.

## When to give up

If the stem at the soil line is mushy and you can pull the plant out of the soil with no resistance, the crown is gone. Take a cutting from healthy upper growth (if any) and propagate; compost the rest.
