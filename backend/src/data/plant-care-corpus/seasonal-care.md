# Seasonal houseplant care

Most houseplants come from places without seasons, but indoor plants still experience seasons because of light and temperature shifts in your home. The two big inflection points are mid-fall and mid-spring.

## Fall (shorter days, heat starts running)

What changes:

- Daylight drops 30-50% between September and December
- Indoor heating drops humidity dramatically (often into the 20s%)
- Plants enter slower growth or dormancy
- Cold drafts from windows become a real risk

What to adjust:

- **Water less often.** Plants in low-growth mode use less water. Wait longer between waterings. A monstera watered weekly in July might want every 2-3 weeks in November.
- **Stop fertilizing** after mid-October. Most plants won't use it and it'll just burn roots.
- **Move plants away from cold windows.** A 2-inch buffer from a single-pane window is enough to avoid the cold draft that drops leaf surface temperature into the 40s overnight.
- **Add a humidifier** for tropicals. The combination of forced-air heat and closed windows wrecks humidity-sensitive plants.
- **Move sun-loving plants closer to windows.** South-facing windows that were too hot in summer are perfect in winter.

## Winter (low light, dry air)

The hardest season for tropicals. Expect:

- Slower growth or none at all (normal — don't panic)
- More leaf drop than usual (also often normal)
- Plants near windows getting cold damage on leaves touching the glass
- Higher pest pressure, especially spider mites (love dry winter air)

What to do:

- **Inspect for pests weekly.** Catching mites at week 1 vs week 4 is the difference between a quick recovery and a slow death.
- **Don't repot or propagate.** Wait for spring.
- **Consider grow lights** if your plants are clearly straining. Most tropicals tolerate the dim months but a few hours of supplemental light a day prevents leggy stretch.

## Spring (longer days, growth restarts)

The opposite of fall. Plants wake up.

- **Resume fertilizing** at half-strength as you see new growth.
- **Increase watering frequency** as the plant accelerates.
- **Repot** anything that needs it before the growth spurt hits in earnest.
- **Propagate** — spring cuttings root the fastest.
- **Watch for leggy stretching** that happened over winter and consider pruning to encourage bushier regrowth.

## Summer (peak growth, watch for sun + heat)

- **Water more often** — pots dry out fast in heat.
- **Watch for sunburn** on plants near south-facing windows; consider a sheer curtain.
- **Fertilize regularly** — every 2-3 weeks.
- **Plants moved outside** for the summer need a slow acclimation. A week in deep shade, then a week in dappled shade, then partial sun. Don't dump an indoor plant into 6 hours of direct sun on day one.

## Plants that should move outside in summer

- Citrus, hibiscus, gardenias, jasmines: thrive outdoors in summer humidity + light.
- Outdoor-style tropicals (bird of paradise, banana plants): same.

## Plants that should stay inside

- Calatheas, marantas, ferns: outdoor wind + variable humidity stresses them.
- Variegated plants: direct sun can burn variegation.
- Sensitive succulents: most actually prefer indoor controlled conditions to unpredictable outdoor weather.

## Bringing plants back inside in fall

Inspect for pests before they come in. Hose off the foliage, check the underside of every leaf, and drench the soil with neem if you suspect anything. One infested plant brought inside in October can spread mites across your collection by January.
