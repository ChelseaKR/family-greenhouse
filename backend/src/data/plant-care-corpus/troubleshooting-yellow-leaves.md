# Yellow leaves — what they mean

Yellow leaves are the most ambiguous signal on a houseplant. They can mean five different things, and the only way to narrow it down is to look at _which_ leaves are yellow and _how_ they got that way.

## Lower leaves only, gradual yellowing

- **Most likely: overwatering**. Roots are suffocating; the plant sheds older leaves to conserve resources. Check the soil — if it's been wet for a week, you've overwatered. Let it dry out completely, then water less often going forward.
- **Also possible: natural shedding**. Mature plants drop their oldest leaves periodically. If it's one leaf a month and the rest of the plant looks happy, ignore it.

## Lower leaves, plant is also wilted, soil is dry

- **Likely: underwatering**. Older leaves go first when the plant is rationing. Water thoroughly and the wilt should resolve in a few hours.

## New growth is pale/yellow

- **Likely: nutrient deficiency**, usually nitrogen or iron. Fertilize at half-strength with a balanced houseplant fertilizer. If the issue is iron specifically (called "chlorosis" — yellow leaves with green veins), it's often a pH problem; switch to filtered water if you're on hard tap water.

## Yellow with brown spots or rings

- **Likely: a fungal or bacterial infection**, often caused by water sitting on leaves overnight. Move the plant to better airflow, stop misting, water only at the soil line.

## All-over yellowing in days, plant looks soft/limp

- **Likely: root rot from overwatering**, or temperature shock. Unpot and inspect roots — healthy roots are white/cream and firm; rotten ones are brown/black and mushy. Cut off the rot, repot in fresh soil, water sparingly.

## Yellow leaves on a plant you just bought

- **Likely: shipping stress** or moving from a greenhouse to your home's drier air. Wait two weeks before assuming anything is wrong. Don't repot a stressed plant.

## When to remove yellow leaves

Once a leaf has yellowed past 30-40%, it's not coming back. The plant can't re-green a leaf. Snip yellow leaves off at the base — it lets the plant put energy into new growth and looks better.

Don't pull. Cut. Pulling can damage the stem.
